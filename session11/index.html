What Mary Oliver's Critics don't understand

For America's most beloved poet,paying attention to nature is a springboard to the scared

By Ruth Frankly

Oliver uses nature as a springboard to the scared


Illustration by Deanna Halsall

"Mary Oliver is saving my life",Paul Chowder,the title character of Nicholson Baker's novel"The Anthologist",scrawls in the margins off Oliver's "New and Selected Poems,Volume One" struggling poet,Chowder iss suffering from a severe case of writer's block.His girlfriend,with whom he's lived for 8 years,has just left him,ostensibly because he has been unable to write long-overdue introduction to a poetry anthology that he has been putting together.For solace and inspiration,he turns to poets who have been his touchstones-Louise Bogan,Theodore Roethke, Sara Teasdale-before discovering Oliver.In her work,he finds consolation:"I immediately left more sure of what I was doing".Of her poems,he says,"They're very simple.And yet each has somthing"

I don't know exactly what a prayer is.

I do know how to pay attention,how to fall down

into the grass,how to kneel down in the grass

how to be idle and blessed,how to stroll through the fields,

which is what I have been doing all day.

The cadenees are almost Biblical."Attention is the beginning of devotion",she urges elsewhere.
